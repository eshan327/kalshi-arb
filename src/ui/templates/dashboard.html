Read me — what P(model) and P(book) mean

P(model) is the model’s estimate of the probability that the official 60-second benchmark index average (the one Kalshi uses over the final minute) finishes above the strike you see in the grid. It is not the same as the YES contract’s market price on Kalshi — it is our own math on synthetic index proxy and vol.

How it’s computed: With more than 60 seconds left to expiry, we use a Levy-style lognormal approximation to the average of 60 future spot samples (Turnbull–Wakeman–style idea). Inside the last 60 seconds, we switch to a collapsed-variance update: seconds already observed are fixed; only the rest is uncertain. σ comes from vol_estimator.py (realized vol on index log returns). The regime line in the status tells you which branch is active.

What the percentage is: A value like 63.2% means “the model assigns about a 63% chance the TWAP settles above the strike.” The bar width is the same number on a 0–100 scale. It is a probability, not a price in cents.

Bar colors (50% rule): For both metrics, green when the shown probability is > 50% (leans YES), red when < 50% (leans NO), gray at 50% or when data isn’t available (e.g. pricer not ready, or no order-book snapshot yet for P(book)).

P(book) is a separate signal from the Kalshi order book (and aggressor trades when wired): we combine OBI (resting bid vs ask imbalance), TFI (recent buy-yes vs sell-yes flow), and MPP (how much the mid moved vs the spread) into a logistic score in (0, 1), shown as a percent. It measures short-term pressure and liquidity, not the index TWAP model. TFI may stay flat until public trade events are connected.

Why both differ: P(model) is spot + vol + time structure; P(book) is what traders are doing on Kalshi right now. They can diverge — e.g. model says fair YES is high while the book is leaning NO, or the reverse.
